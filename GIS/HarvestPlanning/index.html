Forest Management to Support the Conservation of Endangered Species

Protecting Little Brown Bat Habitats during Logging in North-Interior BC

(5 minutes read)

Forest managers must ensure that forest operations do not disrupt ecosystems or habitats of mammals and similar forest-dwelling species, especially if they are Endangered or Threatened. This normally means that careful planning that considers the presence of these species is always a necessity in forestry activities such as timber logging or harvesting. Indeed several provincial and federal conservation regulations enjoin forest and natural resource managers to make adequate provisions in their management plans to protect ecosystems and habitats of forest-dwelling species during operations such as timber logging. In Northern-Interior BC, one species that is constantly threatened by logging activities is the Myotis Lucifugus, commonly called the Little Brown Bat. (For more on this particular species, check out this handout). In this project, I explore options for conserving Myotis lucifugus habitat during a logging operation in the Prince George-Mackenzie-Peace River Forest districts in BC. Some of the main considerations were:

  1. What Biogeoclimatic (BEC) Zone types are Little Brown Bats mostly found in?
  2. What proportion of Little Brown Bats are present in each BEC zone?
  3. How much forest area is non-loggable as a result of the presence of Little Brown Bats?

I use various GIS analysis and workflows to explore these and other considerations in the succeeding sections

Entity Relations & Normalization



The interactions and processes defined in the business rules were used to develop an Entity Relations Model shown above. To eliminate any data duplication and redundancies in the tables created above, I implemented a series of normalization. Coronel & Morris (2016) posit that a Second Normal Form (2NF) occurs when a database table is already in a First Normal Form (1NF) and includes no partial dependencies. In other words, none of the table's attributes are dependent on a portion of the primary key. Similarly, a Third Normal Form (3NF) is reached when a table is already in Second Normal Form (2NF) and contains no transitive dependencies. Following these recommendations, I ensured that all non-key attributes of tables were dependent on their primary keys, and that all tables had single key values with none of them repeating or duplicated. Conclusion and Moving Forward

A relational database for handling interlibrary lending transactions may entail more complexity and detail than what is depicted in this project. The prudence of decentralizing interlending services, especially in large post secondary institutions, is also a subject of intense debate. Indeed, there are some who argue strongly for decentralizing such systems and feel that their costs can be lowered by automation (Vervliet, 1981). These are quite interesting and important debates, and given my enthusiasm about systems librarianship, I plan to continue developing more skills and knowledge about how database systems and technologies may be designed and implemented to help libraries pull resources together, save cost, and improve effieciency of service delivery.

I am currently developing a front-end web application to connect to this project and improve its accessibility. I'm also looking into building a viable database product, perhaps for a non-profit organization or a similar entity committed to improving literacy access for marginalized communities.
